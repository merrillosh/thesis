The Problem


How transcripts can counteract the influence of digital grade portals on U.S. high schools


The last few years have seen the rapid spread of digital grade portals in U.S. high schools, but research on the efficacy of constant access to students' grades remains mixed. There is some evidence that grade portals help keep students accountable and parents informed, improving students' academic performance overall. However, with parents able to constantly evaluate and respond to their child's perceived academic performance, learners are stripped of some level of control over their education.


This project focuses on the power of digital grade representations to tackle these issues of agency. Transcripts serve as a central document for conveying academic performance to students, parents, teachers, admissions officers, and other stakeholders. By altering the way grades are presented in transcripts, can we counteract the perspective encouraged by online grade portals? How and to what degree can the structure of a transcript build learner self-representation and self-advocacy, for all students irrespective of their families' abilities to navigate digital spaces? This project suggests ways to expand upon how grades are presented in order to better incorporate student voices without altering current grading and teaching models.


1

Development


Opportunities for academic self-reflection are crucial to students' development. Participation in the curation of their own transcripts provides students with the tools and space to interpret experiences from inside and outside of the classroom that have influenced their learning. Our transcript should support students as they develop an integrative narrative of self.

2

Compatibility


Transcripts that are designed to capture a complete and authentic picture of each student's academic growth have the potential to improve student-university 'fit'. Our transcript should offer students a variety of ways to communicate their academic performance, thus helping universities to select students who have the values, skills, attitudes, or perspectives they are seeking.

3

Efficiency


With only minutes to read each application, admissions officers could benefit from a curatorial design that directs their attention to students' most important courses and activities. However, evaluators may sometimes require more traditional academic metrics. Our transcript must accomodate both of these needs while maintaining the reliability expected of an official transcript.

Functional Attributes

  • Toggle view - Using this switch, the user can view either the default/student-curated view which includes the classes a student has identified as central to their academic journey, relevant extra-curricular activities, other information regarding their student experience, and a more traditional transcript view which includes a complete list of courses and the grades they received with no supplementary materials
  • Student statement - This short blurb offers student's the opportunity to freely describe themselves as a student or otherwise speak to their academic experience. Its placement on the left-hand side of the transcript encourages evaluators to read it first and use it as a frame, shaping their understanding of the rest of the transcript.
  • Evaluator assistance - These buttons make it easy for users—students, parents, educators, and admissions officers—to learn more about the document. Recording data regarding their usage will also allow us to recognize and improve areas of confusion.
  • Counselor verification & approval - Each student's list of courses and grades is automatically fed into this transcript from school records, just like a traditional transcript, but this feature allows schools to provide verification of the other information included in the student-curated portion of the transcript.
  • Chronological scrolling structure - As this transcript is intentionally designed to reflect students' self-narratives, courses are ordered chronologically and grouped by year with sub-groupings by subject. This means that by reading from left to right, a user gets a sense of how students progressed and developed in their interests over their four years of high school as well as how they plan to continue doing so in the future (the transcript shown here includes semester grades from left to right, but this is easily adapted to schools that use a trimester or quarter system.)
  • Activity labeling - Students have the ability to group and label their experiences—examples include “classes”, “clubs”, and “personal projects”—making the curated view extremely inclusive. Students also have the ability to name and alter the subject groupings of their classes.For example, from a students perspective math and science courses could appear as one category labeled “S.T.E.M.” even if their school doesn't use the same categorizations.
  • Selective info regarding courses - While the traditional transcript view includes all courses and grades a student receives, in the curated view students can highlight different aspects of their courses by selecting what information to show (or omit) from their transcript. For example, this student has included their grades for AP statistics but not for Honors Algebra 2. Allowing students to determine whether they'd like to highlight their participation, improvement, performance, growth, work, or other qualities encourages them to see the benefits of classes beyond good grades.
  • Course-level color coding - Each course is color-coded by level to give the transcript's viewer a visual overview of the areas in which a student improved or pursued more challenging courses. In this example, a reader can quickly see the change from dark blue, to teal, to red in the areas associated with science, math, and the arts, providing an instant sense of both what opportunities a student took advantage of and what areas they explored in greater depth.
  • Multimedia evidence - Allowing students to include a variety of written and visual supplements promotes a broad view of the value of educational experiences beyond hyper fixation of grades. For example, a student who showed resiliance in a course that personally challenged them may wish to submit a statement of support from their teacher or a student who participated in an art class outside of their high school might benefit from showing the work they produced during the course. Allowing students to demonstrate their learning in many different ways encourages students to focus on more than achieving the highest possible grade and it offers admissions officers a fuller picture of student achievement.

A Note On Equity
& Accessibility

While not the central goal of this project, in order to be deemed successful our transcript design must minimize inequities in how students see themselves, depict themselves, and are evaluated. A successful design should mitigate some concerns with how students are currently evaluated by colleges and other institutions after graduation, primarily by allowing a wider range of experiences to be represented. Additionally, any interfaces for transcript creation must be usable for all students regardless of ability.


To ensure that these standards are met, the finished transcript design must meet all Web Content Accessibility Guidelines as set by the W3C. Further testing will be needed to ensure that the redesigned transcript does not disproportionally impact students' admissions negatively or in a way that increases current inequities in college education.

Recommendations for Future Development


Resources and strategies for launching a redesigned transcript on a larger scale


The above testing plan should provide enough evidence of the efficacy of our redesigned high school transcript to justify a small pilot program, allowing real schools to begin using and submitting the transcript to college admissions departments. However, it is only the beginning of the required testing for implementing such a large-scale project. The three stages of testing outlined above focus largely on the experience of individual users with creating and reading the transcript but once the transcript is launched, further stages of assessment will need to be developed focusing on the impacts of this new approach to reporting students' academic performance at a systems level.


In addition to further assessments, the plan for launching the redesigned transcript will require further development after the design has been finalized. In order to be successful, any changes to a process as high stakes as college admissions will require deep buy-in both from both students and their parents and university admissions departments. To achieve the level of credibility required to instill trust in all stakeholders, there are several paths we could explore:

  • Seeking the endorsement of a high-profile educational organization such as the National Educational Association
  • Partnering with a small group of highschools to promote and collect data on the results of using the transcript
  • Applying for grant money to incentivize university involvement in further research
